1 Executive Summary

Predictive PO Delivery Risk is C16's first production AI use case. It combines a deterministic ABAP scoring engine with LLM-powered reasoning to identify open purchase orders that will miss their delivery dates — before it happens — and explain why in specific, actionable terms.

Unlike traditional procurement reports (ME2M, ME2N) that show individual PO statuses, this use case cross-references 6 data dimensions simultaneously: delivery history, change instability, partial receipts, inventory exposure, quality returns, and vendor behavior. The AI discovers patterns that are invisible when these dimensions are viewed in isolation — vendor clusters deteriorating, systematic plant-level receiving failures, escalating quality trajectories, and production-critical supply chain gaps.

3 AI Methodology — What Makes This "AI"

Architecture Philosophy: Hybrid Intelligence

This use case deliberately combines two complementary approaches:

LayerTechnologyWhat It DoesWhy
Layer 1: Deterministic ScoringABAP (weighted linear combination)Scores each PO line 0-100 based on 6 factorsRepeatable, auditable, fast. Same input → same score.
Layer 2: LLM ReasoningLarge Language Model (Claude-class)Cross-references all 6 data blocks, discovers patterns, generates explanationsFinds non-obvious patterns. Explains in human language. Adapts to context.
Why Not Traditional ML?
Traditional ML (Random Forest, XGBoost, LSTM) requires: (a) labeled historical data ("this PO was late" / "this PO was on time"), (b) feature engineering, (c) model training, (d) ongoing model maintenance. Most SAP customers don't have labeled procurement outcome data in a clean, accessible format. The LLM approach works on Day 1 with zero training data — it reasons from the raw signals directly.

AI Models & Algorithms Used

ComponentTechniqueSpecifics
Risk Score (Layer 1)Weighted Linear Combination6 factors with pre-defined weights summing to 100. No ML — pure rule-based arithmetic. See §6 for formula.
Vendor Trend AnalysisPeriod-over-Period ComparisonCompares last 3 months delivery performance vs. prior 9 months. Detects trajectory shifts (improving/worsening). Simple moving average, not ARIMA or exponential smoothing.
Change Instability DetectionFrequency AnalysisCounts changes per PO in CDHDR. Compares against population mean. >3σ = outlier. Also checks time-of-day (night edits = urgency signal).
Quality TrajectoryChronological Pattern AnalysisLLM examines return quantities over time per vendor. Detects escalation (1→5→10→35) vs. stable (10, 10, 10) vs. improving (35→10→5→0).
Cross-Referencing (Layer 2)LLM Reasoning with Structured PromptAnthropic Claude (or GPT-4 class). Temperature 0.3 (low creativity, high precision). 200K token context window. 12 explicit reasoning rules. Stateless — no customer data retained between sessions.
Vendor ClusteringLLM Pattern RecognitionNot k-means or DBSCAN. The LLM identifies vendor clusters by shared failure patterns (same plant, same date range, same symptom). This is reasoning, not unsupervised learning.
Chunking StrategyToken Budget ManagementABAP pre-filters to top 50-100 risk items. LLM receives vendor-by-vendor chunks of ~15K tokens each. Results merged into final output. Prevents context overflow.

Honest Limitations

  • No prediction probability. Unlike trained ML, the LLM doesn't output "83% chance of late delivery." It outputs qualitative assessments: "HIGH risk because of X, Y, Z signals." This is a trade-off: no false precision, but also no ROC curve.
  • Reasoning varies between runs. The same data may produce slightly different explanations (different word choices, different emphasis). The ABAP risk score is deterministic; the AI narrative is not.
  • No learning loop (yet). The system doesn't learn from outcomes. If the AI flags a PO as high-risk and it arrives on time, that feedback isn't incorporated. Phase 2 roadmap includes outcome tracking.
  • Dependent on data quality. If EKBE goods receipt dates are wrong, or CDHDR is incomplete due to archiving, the analysis will be wrong. Garbage in → garbage out applies to LLMs too.

6 Risk Scoring Algorithm

Formula

RISK_SCORE = W1(overdue) + W2(vendor_history) + W3(change_instability) + W4(partial_gr) + W5(quality) + W6(stockout)

Where:
  W1 = Overdue Days Score      (0–30 pts)  → max weight, primary signal
  W2 = Vendor Late History     (0–25 pts)  → >50% late deliveries = max
  W3 = PO Change Instability   (0–15 pts)  → 10+ changes = max
  W4 = Zero/Partial Receipt    (0–15 pts)  → 0% received + overdue = max
  W5 = Quality Returns         (0–10 pts)  → 5+ returns = max
  W6 = Stockout Exposure       (0–5 pts)   → no stock + overdue = max

TOTAL POSSIBLE: 100 points

7 AI Reasoning Engine — 12 Rules (v2)

This is the structured prompt that drives the LLM's analysis. Each rule encodes specific procurement domain expertise as a reasoning constraint. The rules were validated through a live stress test (Step 4) and refined based on 3 identified gaps.

Prompt Template (v2 — Production)

View Full Prompt Template 
You are a procurement risk analyst examining live SAP data.
Analyze the following structured data blocks and identify
delivery risks that a human procurement team might miss.

RULES:
1.  Look for BEHAVIORAL SHIFTS, not just threshold breaches.
    A vendor with 95% on-time dropping to 80% is more concerning
    than a vendor stable at 75%.

2.  Cross-reference ALL data blocks — never analyze in isolation.
    Vendor quality returns MUST be considered when assessing
    that vendor's open PO delivery risk.

3.  For each risk finding, explain WHY in specific terms.
    Cite PO numbers, vendor names, quantities, dates, percentages.
    Never say "there may be a risk" — say "PO 4500004998 has 78
    changes in 12 days, including night-time edits at 22:22"

4.  Recommend SPECIFIC actions per finding.
    Not "monitor this vendor" — but "mandate incoming QI for
    Abbott material R-101 before GR on PO 4500014363"

5.  Surface patterns: vendor clusters deteriorating together,
    material groups with shared delays, seasonal effects,
    user behavior anomalies (night edits, weekend changes)

6.  Rank by BUSINESS IMPACT: value × probability × alternatives.
    A €7M PO with a single vendor and zero receipt is higher risk
    than ten €5K POs from diversified sources.

7.  Distinguish production-critical vs. non-production items.
    Desk pads and cleaning supplies overdue = LOW risk.
    Engine components overdue = CRITICAL risk.

8.  PRODUCTION MATERIALITY: For manufacturing plants, assess
    PRODUCTION LINE IMPACT separately from financial exposure.
    A €5K raw material PO that stops a €5M production line is
    HIGHER risk than a €50K office furniture PO. Check if the
    material is a BOM component.

9.  PARTIAL DELIVERY BRACKET: Don't only flag zero-receipt POs.
    Flag PARTIAL deliveries (50-90% received) that are overdue
    as a SEPARATE risk category — the vendor started but may
    have capacity constraints.

10. SYSTEMATIC VENDOR FAILURE: When a vendor has 3+ open POs
    ALL with zero receipt across DIFFERENT order dates, flag
    this as a VENDOR-LEVEL failure, not individual PO-level.
    Name the vendor and ALL affected POs.

11. SUPPLY CHAIN DEPTH: When ALL vendors for a plant have zero
    receipts from the same date range, the root cause is a
    plant-level receiving problem, not vendor failure. Check
    for systemic GR processing backlogs.

12. DON'T DISMISS LOW-VALUE: 500K units at €0.02/unit is likely
    a production input. Assess its manufacturing chain role
    before classifying as "low risk."

14 Risks & Mitigations
#RiskLikelihoodImpactMitigation
R1 LLM hallucination — fabricated PO/vendor Medium High All findings are grounded in extracted SAP data. The LLM only sees data from the customer's own system. Hallucination audit methodology (§13) catches any drift. Temperature 0.3 minimizes creative fabrication.
R2 Performance timeout on large dataset Medium Medium v2 hardening: P_LKBACK limits EKBE scan, P_MAXHST caps per-vendor records, progress indicator prevents frozen-screen perception. See §16 benchmarks.
R3 Scoring thresholds don't fit customer Low Medium Thresholds are configurable. Calibration guide (§15) provides industry-specific tuning recommendations. Start with defaults, adjust after 2-week pilot.
R4 Change docs not enabled (CDHDR empty) Medium Low Block 3 (change instability) returns empty. AI skips that signal — other 5 blocks still provide value. Recommend enabling change logging for EINKBELEG.
R5 LLM reasoning inconsistency between runs High Low Expected behavior — LLM outputs are non-deterministic. The ABAP risk SCORE is deterministic (same input = same score). The AI narrative adds interpretation. Users should rely on scores for tracking, AI for insights.
R6 User trusts AI blindly — takes action on flawed finding Medium High All findings include "Recommendation" not "Action Taken." Human approval is required before any procurement action. Training materials should emphasize: AI advises, human decides.
